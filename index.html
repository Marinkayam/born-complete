The product designer that's born complete

Design, code, and AI aren't three skills bolted together on my CV. They're one way of working — and it has been converging toward this role since 2008. Every messy domain, every rule still being written, all governed from a single live system.

Let's talk What does that mean?

What does born complete mean?

I wasn't retrofitted for this.

Most designers arrived at AI the way legacy platforms arrived at it: as a patch. A course, a plugin, a new tab in the toolbar. The method underneath stayed exactly where it was.

Mine didn't need patching, because the method was always the point. I've been building systems since I was drawing data for Haaretz in 2008 — structure first, artifact second. Nothing got bolted on later; it was all in the sheet from the start. When generative AI showed up, it didn't replace the way I work. It ran on it. Same graph, more throughput.

That's why I don't just use LLMs — I teach them, as a Gen AI lecturer at the College of Management, on the same weeks I'm shipping AI-assisted design at Monto. Designed and built from the ground up to handle the product as it exists now, and as it evolves.

Radically centralized

From the first research question to the component that ships in production — based on intent and context, not handoff.

Built as one

One system, one source of truth.

At Monto I own the design system as production infrastructure — shared components, states and tokens running across both Current Gen and Next Gen. Not a Figma library that drifts. The singular base layer.
